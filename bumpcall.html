Overview

Stage #2 startup bumpcall sought to redefine the approach to communication and needed Digital Design Interns to make that happen.

That's where I came in. bumpcall's goal was to change communication by turning "bumps on the street" to five-minute FaceTime calls. The founders realized that there was a need to change the way we talk, to decrease the looming anxiety of waiting for texts and not knowing when to call. Through the pandemic, bumpcall was born. Joining bumpcall early on, I made the most of my knowledge from hackathon and designathon competitions and saw this as an opportunity to put my UI/UX design skills to work through countless iterations.

My Contribution

As one of two interns for a startup, I took on a big position as someone who would make significant design decisions. Weekly, we would discuss with one of the cofounders and run through new iterations of pieces of the app we designed. Given that the mobile app only had a few iterations of the MVP to begin with, I was able to design numerous pages, states, components, and iterations of the product. As my junior spring semester came to a close, I was brought on as Director of Product Design briefly to continue my work with the designs before finally parting ways.

Our Solution

With only a few prototyped screens ranging from opening the app to "bumping into someone", my job as an intern was to redesign the entire app. Researching the psychology and cognitive processes of friendships, of reaching out to someone in-person and digitally, and of running into someone, our solution was to bring new life to the product with new colors, new interactions, and new conversations.

Past Iterations

Challenges

Born out of boredom, bumpcall aims to address the consistent and ever-growing anxiety of digital communication. As an ambitious, enthusiastic, and young user experience designer, bumpcall was my first opportunity into the industry as an intern. At first, I felt like there was a lot riding on this, even though it was unpaid.


Beyond my personal desires and challenges, some initial challenges with bumpcall was the disagreement of what the bumpcall brand was to become. There was simply an overwhelming amount of orange and the wireframe designs felt a bit dated, so communicating suggestions for redesigns was met with resistance by the lead Product Designer who was one of the co-founders. While neither of us had formal design experience, I had collections of past designs from competitions to justify my ideas and eventually meet in the middle with the co-founder.

Research

Audience

Upon onboarding and discussing the purpose of the product, it came to my mind that bumpcall would best serve the college student population, followed by students in general. This young demographic would be best suited to pitch bumpcall to because of students' daily interactions with each other, seeing each other on campus, in the hallways, libraries, dining halls, study lounges, on the sidewalks, etc. Given that isolation due to COVID-19 was one of the factors that gave rise to bumpcall, students have been one of the most impacted demographics with classes being held virtually and most colleges and universities opting out of reopening campus.


The premise of bumpcall is to bridge this loss of social interaction and communication via a spontaneous 5-minute video call to imitate that face-to-face coincidental meetup. I find that bumpcall's "bump" feature best fits the student culture and atmosphere due to the frequency and carefree nature of those in real-life interactions. Bumping on bumpcall is a social interaction, not a business meeting.

Questions

Competitive Analysis

While I understood that the co-founders had done their research, I still didn't understand a few things that made this product stand out. The initiative was a great idea, but I wondered how this was in market demand.


"What made this different from sporadically calling or FaceTiming my friends?"

"There's so many messaging platforms so are we entering a saturated market head-on?"

"Is it possible to hit the bump and just not bump into anyone?"



How might we...

Accumulating my research together, I drafted some "How might we..." statements to drive the project in the right direction.


"How might we differentiate ourselves from FaceTime?"

"How might we connect more people together who might not be the best of friends?"

"How might we invite more users to the product or get users to share with friends?"

Goal


How do we bring users together in a way that connects people in a spontaneous "bump on the street"-like fashion amidst the pandemic?

Key Takeaways

When mapping out the user flow of the different routes one might take with bumpcall, it came to our attention that, like introverts and extroverts, we would have users who will be making the calls and users who would receive the calls.


This distinction became exceptionally clear when the initial prototype had a plain spinning circle upon hitting the bump button in the middle. Users initiating the bump were told that they wait between 20 seconds to several minutes. That is a prime opportunity to make the most out of, but should be limited given that the wait is so variable.


What should a user do in this "waiting room" state?

How can we keep a user engaged with the product here while they wait for someone to bump into?
